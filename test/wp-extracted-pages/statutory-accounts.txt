					<h2>Statutory accounts don’t go wrong suddenly — they fall out of sync
</h2>				
		<p> </p><p>Most businesses don’t ignore their numbers.</p><p>Transactions are recorded. Invoices go out. Costs are tracked. Bank activity is visible.</p><p>On the surface, everything looks complete.</p><p>The problem is not whether data exists.<br /> The problem is whether everything still connects when it needs to.</p><p>Statutory accounts bring everything together into one position — and that’s where gaps appear.</p><p>Figures that seemed fine during the year no longer align.<br /> Balances don’t reconcile exactly.<br /> Certain transactions need to be revisited because they were recorded in a way that doesn’t hold under statutory reporting rules.</p><p>That’s the point where statutory accounts stop being routine and become something that requires proper handling — because once submitted, they are final, public, and relied upon.</p>		
					<h2>Statutory Accounts UK – Built Around Accuracy, Structure and Compliance
</h2>				
		<p> </p><p>Statutory accounts are formal financial statements prepared under UK statutory accounting principles and filed with Companies House.</p><p>They are not internal summaries. They are the official version of your financial position.</p><p>Our statutory accounts preparation focuses on one outcome:</p><p>your accounts must hold together — technically, legally, and logically — before they are filed.</p><p>That means:</p><p>       ●   figures align with underlying records</p><p>       ●   disclosures are complete</p><p>       ●   formatting meets statutory requirements</p><p>       ●   and the final output reflects the business as it actually operates</p>

					<h2>Statutory Accounts Format and Technical Requirements
</h2>				
		<p>Statutory accounts must follow a defined format.</p><p>That includes:</p><p>       ●   consistent presentation</p><p>       ●   correct application of accounting                     standards</p><p>       ●   required disclosures and notes</p><p>The detail here matters more than it appears.</p><p>For example:</p><p>       ●   director loan accounts must be                        treated correctly</p><p>       ●   accruals and prepayments must                      reflect actual timing</p><p>       ●   fixed asset values must be supported              properly</p><p>These are not cosmetic adjustments. They change how the business is presented.</p>		
					<h2>Where Statutory Accounts Become Complicated
</h2>				
		<p>Statutory accounts become difficult when:<br />       ●   transactions span multiple periods<br />       ●   directors take mixed salary and dividends<br />       ●   costs are recorded without clear categorisation<br />       ●   intercompany transactions exist<br />       ●   prior period errors carry forward<br />These are not rare scenarios. They are common in growing businesses.<br />A common example is where director loan balances are recorded inconsistently across the year, creating discrepancies at year-end. Another is where income is recognised in one period but costs are recorded in another, leading to misaligned profit reporting. <br />And this is where a “simple accounts preparation” approach breaks down — because the work is no longer about compiling figures, but about correcting structure.</p>
